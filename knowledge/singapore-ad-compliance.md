# Singapore healthcare advertising compliance

The hard rail for every angle, headline, script, and static. Source: Healthcare Services (Advertisement) Regulations 2021 under the Healthcare Services Act (HCSA), administered by MOH, plus the Medicines (Advertisement and Sale) Act (MASA) and the Health Products Act (HPA) administered by HSA, and the Singapore Code of Advertising Practice (SCAP) administered by ASAS. This is a working summary, not legal advice. The doctor (the licensee) is legally responsible and signs off every ad. When unsure, verify against the primary regulation or ask the clinic's compliance contact.

Reference: Healthcare Services (Advertisement) Regulations 2021, Singapore Statutes Online (sso.agc.gov.sg). Penalty for breach can include a fine and licensing consequences for the clinic.

## Who this applies to

- Applies to advertisements of **licensable healthcare services** by an HCSA **licensee** (the clinic or doctor) or an authorised person. Only a licensee or their authorised person may advertise the service.
- Aesthetic and medical clinic services fall under this. Non-licensable services (for example TCM, physiotherapy) fall under other laws such as MASA, not these regulations.
- If the clinic advertises in conjunction with a product (for example a face cream), the ad is still subject to these regulations.

## The hard NO list (never produce these)

1. **No before-and-after images or video. No after-only images or video** of a person's appearance or feature relating to a treatment. This is the single biggest constraint for aesthetics. (Showing such images to a patient during an in-person or existing-patient consultation is allowed, but not in an ad.)
2. **No testimonials, reviews, or endorsements** about the service or any employee, except the narrow statutory exception (Regulation 14), which is tightly conditioned. Default to none.
3. **No laudatory statements or superlatives.** Banned in spirit: best, number one, No.1, leading, top, finest, unrivalled, unique, most advanced, safest, painless, guaranteed, miracle, world-class, premier, superior, cutting-edge, and similar praise words.
4. **No results claims or unjustified expectations.** Nothing implying the clinic gets results "not achievable by other clinics", nothing creating an expectation the treatment cannot reliably meet.
5. **No comparison or deprecation.** Do not compare or contrast quality with other clinics, do not put down other clinics or their services.
6. **No soliciting or encouraging.** The ad must not be written in a manner that solicits or encourages the public to use the service. This is subtle: a hard "book your procedure now" push reads as soliciting. Ads inform and qualify, they do not pressure.
7. **Nothing exaggerated, false, misleading, or deceptive.** Every claim must be factually accurate and capable of being substantiated.
8. **Nothing offensive, ostentatious, or in bad taste**, and nothing that undermines the dignity of the medical, dental, or nursing profession.
9. **No unrealistic speed or convenience claims** (for example "treatment done in one visit", "teleconsult in 60 seconds") unless factually true for the general case.
10. **Medical-device claims** (if a device is named) must not say 100% safe, no side effects, miraculous, guaranteed, or a sure cure, and must be substantiated.

## What you CAN do (the compliant lane)

- **Educate.** Explain the condition, the science, the mechanism, what the treatment involves, who it suits, recovery, risks. Factual and specific.
- **Inform about the doctor's real, substantiable point of difference.** Credentials, accreditations, technique, process, technology used, factually stated, without superlatives.
- **Answer real questions** the market is asking about the treatment.
- **Frame around the mechanism and process** (this fits Jeremy's sophisticated-market play well, and it is compliant because it is factual and educational).
- **Qualify, not pressure.** Invite the right person to learn more or to a consultation, softly, without soliciting language. A consultation offer is fine; a hard-sell of the procedure is not.
- Include the date of publication where required (brochures, leaflets, flyers, pamphlets).

## How this shapes the 25 to 30 angles

Because before-and-afters, testimonials, superlatives, and results claims are all off the table, the angles must win on **education, mechanism, specificity, and the doctor's real authority**. That is harder, and it is exactly why a compliant creative engine is the moat. Every angle should be checked against the hard NO list before it is written up. Mark any angle you drop and why.

## The compliance check (run on every output)

For each angle, headline, script line, and static, confirm:
- No before/after or after-only treatment imagery.
- No testimonial, review, or endorsement.
- No superlative or laudatory word.
- No results claim or unjustified expectation.
- No comparison or deprecation.
- Not written to solicit or pressure.
- Every factual claim substantiable by the clinic.

If any check fails, revise or drop. Log dropped items in the client output so the doctor can see what was cut and why.

## Outside Singapore

The equivalent regulation exempts advertising directed at a country outside Singapore. If a specific campaign targets only a non-Singapore audience, the Singapore content rules may not bind, but you must then apply that country's rules. Do not assume. For any Singapore-resident audience, apply this file in full.
